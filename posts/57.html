An Evaluation of Cloud Agent Development Tools

Published May. 5, 2025

Introduction

Since the release of OpenAI’s GPT-3 model, there has been a rush to develop frameworks, platforms, and tools to aid in the development of AI, LLM, and ML backed solutions. Over the past three weeks, I have spent time with Amazon’s Bedrock, Azure’s AI Foundry, Google’s Dialogflow CX and Vertex AI. As my research time was limited, I focused on documentation, UI, and three powerful features which are present in most of these products: Agents, Flows, and Knowledge Bases.

Originally, I intended to evaluate Microsoft Azure’s AI Foundry along with the products from both Amazon and Google. However, AI Foundry was generally released about two weeks ago, as of the time of writing, and I cannot fairly evaluate it in its current state. Many of its features are marked with the “PREVIEW” designation and I frequently ran into UI problems which prevented me from using the product as intended. I was pleasantly surprised at the quality of AI Foundry’s documentation and particularly enjoyed the ability to select which programming language to display the code examples in. Additionally, the UI is clean, simple, easy to use, and I believe that it will eventually become a worthwhile product.

As a side note, both Dialogflow CX and Vertex AI are soon™ being consolidated into Google’s Conversational Agents console. The terminology and product names, used here, may change.

Documentation

Documentation is important. Poorly documented projects are difficult to learn and adopt, frustrating to work with, and will cost your company and/or clients a significant amount of money in the long run. The skills and experience required to write good documentation are not developed overnight, and documentation is often thrown to the wayside in-favour of more rapid development.

With massive financial backing and access to the world’s top talent, industry giants such as Amazon, Google, and Microsoft have no excuse for providing poor quality documentation. There should be no tolerance for it.

Google’s Dialogflow CX is where my research began. Its documentation is somewhat good, and I found it to be both accessible and readable. Features often provided links to their associated documentation, and it is organized in a way which makes it easy to navigate without resorting to the search field. The documentation uses simple, clear language with heavy use of numbered lists and links to other pages and documentation. However, there is a lack of full, working examples and it can be difficult to test that certain features work without a fair bit of experimentation and guesswork. As the product is still receiving updates, I have hope that their documentation will further improve over time.

Similarly to Dialogflow CX’s documentation, Google’s Vertex AI suffers from a lack of full, working examples. Its documentation is also scattered, missing information, and tedious to learn from. When I first approached the product, I thought that it had a supported Java SDK even though the documentation only gave examples using its Python SDK. These two pages linked to these two GitHub repositories which host two recently updated Java SDKs. What is the difference between them, what are they capable of, why are they in separate documentation areas, and how can I use them with Vertex AI? I do not know, because the documentation did not tell me. This was a recurring theme as I tried to learn and use Vertex AI, and it was frustrating.

An interesting product, which I stumbled upon while playing with Vertex AI, is Google’s Colab. It is a free, hosted version of Jupyter Notebook on Google’s Cloud Platform. As a fan of Jupyter Notebook, I had a great experience with Colab and it allowed me to quickly run the code in Vertex AI’s documentation. Though Colab is a capable platform to develop an agent with, I was unable to find any documentation describing how to deploy an agent on Vertex AI from it. This seems like a missed opportunity as it could help to streamline development.

As I once worked for an Amazon Business Partner, I am biased towards their documentation. I hold it in high regard as a standard for how accessible, detailed, and readable that cloud provider documentation can be. I am sure that their documentation has its faults, but I have not yet run into any notable problems.

Bedrock’s documentation was a breath of fresh-air, after the frustration I felt while working with Vertex AI. As with Dialogflow CX, Bedrock often provides direct links to a feature’s associated documentation, it is organized for easy navigation, its language is clear, and there are relevant links to other sections of the documentation. The key difference is that Bedrock’s documentation appears much more thorough and detailed. I came out of the Bedrock documentation with far fewer questions than I did with either of Google’s services.

User Interface/Experience

Dialogflow CX seems to have originally been created for the low-code development of telephony systems. As these systems can benefit from the use of LLMs, Google has rightfully incorporated them into their pre-existing product. At a high level, Dialogflow CX appears capable of everything that Bedrock is. However, Azure’s AI Foundry (2025), Amazon’s Bedrock (2023), and Vertex AI (2019) were released much more recently than the original Dialogflow (2014).They have had the benefit of learning from its mistakes. All of this is to say that Dialogflow CX is powerful, but appears hampered by old design decisions which have led to a cumbersome user interface (UI) and user experience (UX).

Unlike documentation, the UI and UX of large products can become difficult to change as time goes on. Updating them can sometimes cause confusion and even outrage in longtime users, and it can be seen as a development burden which may not be worth investing in. From the perspective of someone who has just been introduced to Dialogflow CX, I believe that it needs more love, care, and effort put into its UI and UX if it hopes to continue competing with Bedrock and AI Foundry. Google is a multi-billion-dollar company and they can afford the resources required to accomplish this.

As an example of Dialogflow CX’s cumbersome design, look at the flow feature which both it and Bedrock offer. Both products have a node-based UI. In my experience, this type of UI usually allows the user to add or drag-and-drop new nodes onto a canvas, drag and rearrange existing nodes, and connect nodes together by dragging a line between one node’s output and another’s input. Bedrock follows this pattern, but Dialogflow CX does not.

In Dialogflow CX, the user must navigate through multiple, nested menus to create, configure, and connect nodes together. There is seemingly no affordance for the user to interactively perform these actions. This is a common theme for the UI and UX of Dialogflow CX’s features, and it greatly increases the cognitive load required to work with them. In comparison, Bedrock’s UI and UX is much more straightforward in that it simplifies Flow development by enabling the expected interactive actions. While Bedrock also makes use of nested menus to configure nodes once they have been selected, the menus are far less cluttered and are easier to understand. Care has been put into how Bedrock’s menu and configuration options are laid out, with irrelevant configuration options being hidden until the user makes specific selections which require them to be shown.

With their more recent releases, AI Foundry, Bedrock, and Google’s own Vertex AI have not suffered from the UI and UX problems present in Dialogflow CX. While visually distinct, each of them has a straightforward UI and provides a relatively painless UX. From a developer’s standpoint, I have no complaints worth mentioning.

Agents

Agents, known as Playbooks in Dialogflow CX, are configurable LLMs which are generally capable of data retrieval and storage, calling functions and tools, working with APIs which support the OpenAPI specification, and retrieval augmented generation (RAG).

A conversation between a user and an agent is called a session. Each product enables its agents to resume past sessions and to refer to information within them. Both Bedrock and Dialogflow CX can save a session for up to 24 hours or 365 days respectively. Optionally, Bedrock can generate and store a summary of the conversation instead. Of the three products, Vertex AI offers the most flexibility with its session management API and it may also be able to save a session indefinitely, as its documentation does not specify a time to live.

Though their session management features are similar, the products diverge in their ability to call functions, tools, and in how they use OpenAPIs. They each appear equivalently capable, but the amount of work required to achieve the same goal in each is highly variable and depends on your specific use case.

Google’s products provide numerous methods of integration with 3rd party services such as Oracle, Paypal, Salesforce, and Stripe. Integrations that an agent can directly, dynamically use are called tools. Dialogflow CX is limited in its selection of tools, whereas Vertex AI offers seemingly limitless integrations through its support for OpenAPI, MCP, and other tools. While Bedrock does not currently appear to offer 3rd party integrations VIA tools, it does allow you to write them yourself. Bedrock also supports agent-controlled use of the user’s computer as well as the ability to generate and run code to dynamically respond to a user’s prompt.

In addition to tools, all agents can connect to multiple knowledge bases for RAG. The lack of a specific tool integration in a product may be compensated for by its support for that service in building a knowledge base that the agent can make use of.

Knowledge Bases

A knowledge base, known as a datastore in Dialogflow CX or corpus in Vertex AI, allow agents to perform RAG by providing them with data VIA a defined interface such as those used to connect with vector, SQL, and graph databases. Common data sources include folders and files, scraped websites, and data pulled from a 3rd party such as JIRA or Salesforce.

As with agents, there are many differences in the supported data sources, file types, and in both parsing and chunking of the data. With so many differences, it is infeasible to list them here while also keeping them up to date with future changes. Flows

Flows are state machines which perform a set of actions to achieve a goal. The allowed set of actions varies with each cloud provider. You can typically chain-together multiple agents in-sequence to converse and retrieve data from a user, to call other services to perform actions, to transform data VIA code or an LLM, and to retrieve and store data.

Both Bedrock and Dialogflow CX support flows and provide a node-based UI for creating and editing them. Aside from the differences mentioned above, their flow features are similar and similarly capable. Vertex AI does not have this feature, but its agents are fully programmable and can perform a flow-like series of steps.

Deployment

In Bedrock and Dialogflow CX, agents and flows can be versioned and multiple varying versions deployed alongside each other. Knowledge Bases do not appear to have this ability, but they can sometimes be periodically, automatically updated depending on the source of their data. Vertex AI does not explicitly support versioning, but it appears possible as two agents can be deployed with two differing codebases.

Though each product requires a different set of steps for deployment (Bedrock, Dialogflow CX, Vertex AI), they are similar overall. You define which version of an agent or flow to deploy, follow the deployment steps, and then update your integration to use the new deployment.

Conclusion

If you are developing a telephony system or a simple text-based chat bot, then I would consider Dialogflow CX along with additional research into other products in that domain. I hesitate to recommend it for other use cases as its documentation, UI, and UX make it difficult to reason about and work with. For all other use cases, I recommend evaluating Bedrock or Vertex AI.

For freeform development with few restrictions, Vertex AI is an appropriate choice. Its agents are entirely programmatic, so you should be able to do nearly anything with it. Its various integrations and supporting products also make it an appealing choice.

Amazon’s Bedrock is my go-to recommendation overall, but I would ideally love to use a product which combines Bedrock’s documentation and node-based flow creation UI with Vertex AI’s integrations and the telephony support of Dialogflow CX.
